What Is TrES-2b?

TrES-2b is an ultra–hot Jupiter about 750 light-years away in Draco. It orbits extremely close to its star, reaching temperatures above 1,000°C.

At such high heat, reflective clouds cannot form, leaving the atmosphere exposed and unstable. Its size, extreme proximity, and intense irradiation make it very different from Jupiter and ideal for studying harsh exoplanet atmospheres.

ORBIT: 0.03 AU
TEMP: >1000°C

Why Its Darkness Is Surprising

TrES-2b reflects less than 1% of the light that hits it — darker than coal or soot.

Gas giants usually look bright because of reflective clouds (like Jupiter), so finding a Jupiter-sized planet that absorbs nearly all visible light is unexpected. This makes TrES-2b the darkest exoplanet ever measured.

How We Measure Its Darkness

  • Observed by the Kepler Space Telescope
  • Phase curve analysis tracks brightness over the orbit
  • Compare light from the system when the dayside faces us
  • Result: almost no visible reflection

Why It’s So Dark

The atmosphere contains strong light-absorbing molecules like sodium, potassium, and titanium oxide. High temperatures destroy bright clouds and leave the atmosphere clear but highly absorbing. With no reflective particles and intense heating, the planet absorbs nearly all incoming visible light.

Thermal Glow

Even though it’s dark in visible light, TrES-2b glows faintly in infrared because of its heat. Kepler detects a small increase in brightness when the hot side faces Earth. This glow comes from thermal emission, not reflected starlight.

What This Teaches Us

  • Shows extreme diversity among gas giants
  • Some atmospheres can be cloud-free and highly absorbing
  • Chemistry and temperature can suppress reflectivity
  • Helps refine models for ultra-hot exoplanets
  • Expands our understanding of atmospheric physics
